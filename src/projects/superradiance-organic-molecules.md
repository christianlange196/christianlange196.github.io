## Overview

This project demonstrated the first observation of superradiant and subradiant states in lifetime-limited organic molecules through laser-induced tuning. By coupling dibenzoterrylene (DBT) molecules embedded in an anthracene crystal to a shared photonic mode, we achieved controlled collective emission behavior at cryogenic temperatures.

## Approach

The experiment relies on several key components working together:

- **Nanobeam photonic crystal cavities** fabricated in silicon nitride ($\text{Si}_3\text{N}_4$), designed to enhance light-matter coupling at the single-photon level
- **Cryogenic confocal microscopy** at 2 K to access the lifetime-limited linewidths of individual DBT molecules
- **Laser-induced spectral tuning** to bring pairs of molecules into mutual resonance with sub-linewidth precision

When two molecules are tuned into resonance through a shared cavity mode, their emission rates deviate from independent behavior. The superradiant state emits faster than either molecule alone, while the subradiant state is suppressed — a direct signature of quantum coherence between the emitters.

## Key Result

The collective decay rate for the superradiant state scales as:

$$\Gamma_{\text{super}} = \Gamma_0 + \Gamma_{\text{coll}}$$

where $\Gamma_0$ is the single-molecule decay rate and $\Gamma_{\text{coll}}$ arises from the cavity-mediated dipole-dipole interaction. We measured enhanced and suppressed decay rates consistent with theoretical predictions for two coupled quantum emitters.

## Impact

This work opens a pathway toward building many-body quantum optical systems using solid-state emitters. The combination of molecular photostability, narrow linewidths, and nanophotonic integration makes organic molecules a compelling platform for exploring collective quantum phenomena in scalable architectures.
