The atomic bombing had a profound impression on Salvador Dalí. After the event, he embarked on a movement he called "nuclear mysticism." Artistically, this style was characterized by a combination of classical painting and cubism.

In his studies for *Christ of Saint John of the Cross*, Dalí wrote

>In the first place, in 1950, I had a "cosmic dream" in which I saw this image in colour and which in my dream represented the "nucleus of the atom." This nucleus later took on a metaphysical sense; I considered it "the very unity of the universe," the Christ!

This dream includes an example of what I term *category collapse.* This common dream phenomenon is characterized by the phenomenological breakdown of the distinction between two completely separate ideas or objects.

As a simple example, I regularly have dreams that include one of my little siblings. At one point in the dream, it may be my younger brother Micah. At another point, the same character will instead be Isaiah or Noah. This lack of concrete identity feels normal in the dream because, for the purposes of the dream, there might as well be no distinction. The character in the dream is really about an abstract idea, and that idea can be equally well represented by any of my little siblings.

While this conceptual fluidity feels normal in a dream, it is often hard to explain consciously. This is what Dalí is grasping at when he says "I considered the nucleus very unity of the universe, the Christ!" That is to say that, in his dream, Christ and the nucleus are *the same thing*.

The unconscious identification of Christ with the nucleus of the atom has profound implications. It is a similar statement to saying that Christ is the sun. He is the thing at the center of all things. He is that which holds everything together. As Christ says in the Gospel of John, in a fashion which is particularly striking in view of Dalí's painting,

>And I, when I am lifted up from the earth, will draw all people to myself. \[John 12:32 (ESV)\]

Given that Dalí's fascination with the atomic nucleus began with the detonation of the atomic bomb, the more pressing question is *what happens when Christ dies?* When a sun dies, it is a supernova. When the atom is split, it is a bomb of unbelievable destruction. When Christ breathes his last,

>The curtain of the temple was torn in two, from top to bottom. And the earth shook, and the rocks were split. \[Matt 27:51 (ESV)\]

Like the atomic bomb, the crucifixion is a supernova of unprecedented power, a herald of a new age, after which the world will never be the same.
